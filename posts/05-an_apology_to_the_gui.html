An Apology to the GUI

I own an apology to the GUI. Specifically to GUI-based statistical software. It turns out, you were not as evil as I thought and there might be a future where we can coexist.

Jokes aside. I have never been a fan of point-and-click statistical software like SPSS or Excel. For start, these software are often proprietary meaning that (a) cannot be easily accessed outside work without an expensive subscription, and (b) you are at the mercy of the owners. If the company collapses or retire the software, say goodbye to your ability to analyse or re-analyse data. Additionally, GUI-based tools hide the machinery behind the analysis encouraging researchers to apply solutions that they might not fully (sometimes even not at all) understand. All you need to do is learning the correct sequence of buttons to press. And when you get the results? Don't worry to much about the relevance of all the output tables you get, report all of them. Or none. Or a random selection. Learning the meaning of each table is too time consuming. You have no time.

That is why I have always advocated for programming. Programming forces you to have a more intimate relationship with your data, your models, your tests. You see how the raw data is transformed and prepared before being investigated. You see how analytical tests are used. If a test is not implemented and you are skilled enough you can implement it yourself. The sky is your limit. Additionally, programming does not mean you need to write every single analysis from scratch. Chances are some smart people have already done the work for you, and have offered it for free in a convenient package. Looking to run a linear model? No need to solve matrix operations by hand, you can use in R, or in Python. Interested in Bayesian models (you should)? No need to implement Markov Chain Monte Carlo procedures yourself. That is to say, although I believe that implementing something by hand is important for learning, coding with the usage of packaged functions offers a nice middle-ground between convenience and understanding.

Yet programming is harder than GUIs. I met many people in the past years telling me that they don't want to rely on code because it's too hard for them, they don't have time to learn, being a software engineer is not their job. While the last two seem to me problematic excuses, I agree with the first point. Programming is harder. Leaving aside the fact you need to learn a language, it constantly reminds you what you don't know. Your current limits. And to me, this is important because it forces me to be untrustworthy. But this is a topic for another time.

So, for a long time GUI-based systems have been the option for those who don't want to bother with code. Then ChatGPT came about, followed by others such Copilot an Claude; and things changed. Suddenly, more and more people are switching to code for their research and analyses. I should be happy. My side won. GUIs are moving back. Long live the code.

But I am not. I am not technically against AI in toto, if not for ethical issues. But I see problems developing with researchers vibe coding their work without spending time learning how to do the same work themselves first. Despite the word of AI enthusiasts saying that with AI we will be more productive and solve long-standing questions, I think AI will just exacerbate systemic issues we have in research.

As pointed out almost 20 years ago by Matthew Kirschenbaum in the topical article Hello Worlds, coding languages are languages which express ideas, beliefs, biases, opinions of the writer (programmer). If you are not convinced, consider that coding has a syntax, it expresses ideas, and it could have an effect in the real world. Like a language. So, as a language, it takes time to learn and understand it. AI allows one to cheat the system and convert some thoughts into a language they do not know well or at all. But can you spot the problem with this? If you don't speak the language, how can you tell if the translation is correct? More problematically, in my experience, AI generates code with a complex structure. If you did not develop good programming skills, you won't be able to vet the output you obtained. Maybe you can get a general idea of the program, but you might miss some crucial nuances that could completely change the way you interpret the results. Using the language translation example again. If you ask me "how the show was" and I reply "Lo show e' terrifico", you might think I said "The show was terrific". It was good, a nice show. That would be completely wrong.

So, as more researchers switch to vibe coding projects, we are fostering a community that is unable to have a proper understanding of what they are saying and to verify other's people work. Sure, you can ask an LLM to explain a script for you. All this does, is offloading research work and thinking to a system that is incapable of thoughts. To me, this is a trap. It creates a dependency between individual thoughts and tools under the control of private companies. The freedom of independent thinking is reduced. In cases were there is almost total reliance on AI, this freedom is completely removed. We are trading the freedom of science for convenience. The ability to think, for a ill-defined concept of productivity.

Considering programming as a way to express of thoughts is crucial. There is a certain agreement (at least publicly) that AI-generated papers, essays, and written works in general are problematic and should not be tolerated. I think the reason for this is that these works do not express someone's thoughts, reasoning, understanding. Programming should be treated the same. You could use some AI tool to clean up the final product or standardise the syntax, but I want to see your thoughts, your reasoning, your understanding. A problem can be solved in many different ways. I want to see your solution and not a probabilistic series of words.

As stated above, this does not mean that you need to do everything from scratch. Use packages, use freely-available code. Use answers from Stack Overflow. Including someone's else code in your program is not an issue as long as (a) you made the conscious decision to do so, (b) there is a way to track down the source of the code, (c) you understand what the code does. AI violates all these points. You did not decide how the script works. You did not decide the code that goes into the script. Chances are you don't understand what the code does. This is true especially for those people who vibe code without knowing how to program themselves. How can you understand a language if you don't speak the language?

This is a point that some people who used to code and turned to vibe coding (or relying heavily on AI) seem to miss. In their case, they might be able to understand the output of an LLM. If they are diligent, they check it, assess it, stress test it (as I said, I think AI could have a place, though limited). But they can do this only because the built up the skills required to program. They spent time learning the language. Even so, though, there is the potential risk of overstepping your own skills, trusting AI for tasks you are unequipped to understand properly.

So why people are bothering with using AI to generate code that might be confusing and they might be unable to explain instead of using more established GUI-based analytical tools? Although these tools might not require a deep understanding of the analyses your are running, they at least require you to decide which buttons to press and it is clear who generated the code running in the background (though we might not have full access to it). I think there are two main answers to this question, one more charitable, the other less so. They are not mutually exclusive and I think in most cases they both apply simultaneously.

The first answer, the charitable one, is convenience. Or better, the false sensation of convenience. Now that AI is embedded in many tools, people might use it for many tasks. From replying to emails (what a waste of computational power), to summarise papers (another problematic trend), to writing. If this is the case, people might be more inclined to use AI for programming too. At the end of the day programming is a form of writing. Why should it be the only activity where active thinking and engagement is required? The convenience of AI means you can be more productive, focusing your attention to all the other research tasks you are likely now using AI to complete.

The second answer, less charitable, is ego. Producing code allows you to show off technical skills. Scripts allows you to imbue a sense of proficiency to your work. Unfortunately it's all a bluff. A bluff that fools predominantly those who would not be able to code without AI. I'm seeing more often people asking for help with their programming projects being unable to describe what their code does or is is supposed to do, only to reveal after some pushing that it was generated by an LLM. And this is an huge problem. When you only relay on AI, you cut humans out of your workflow. You become dependent on the probabilistic model because no one knows, not even the model itself, being it incapable of knowing, what its code does. The LLM might give you an explanation, it might be correct, but you'll never know.

Now, I don't want to pass as a saint. Have I used AI for some of my projects? Absolutely. Have I been tempted of using it for something I could not understand? Yes. When I was working on some data modelling replicating some analyses, I encountered an issue for which I could not find an answer (the original paper did not provide details and I think ignored just ignore it). I asked ChatGPT . Surely enough I got an answer. Could I trust it? No. I had no clue whether it was correct. Was I able to test it (I have done this with other AI generated pieces of code)? No, because although I could understand the code, I did not have a good understanding of the problem and whether the solution was correct. The alternative? Ask the experts. The discussion is public and you can see it here. Note that I tried to explain as best as I could the problem, even generating a toy example of data. I also owned the use of ChatGPT . What the experts did? They ignored the AI generated code and tried providing solutions as concepts, not ready-to-use code. They also tried to understand the details, something AI never does (it hardly ever asks for clarifications). What I did at the end was to follow the experts. I accepted I had no idea how to solve this issue (to my defence, even a statistic consultant did not find a solution), and used my knowledge, understanding and skills to translate the experts' answer into code by hand.

So, if you are not keen to learn some programming, please, use the GUI. It's there, it's friendly, it only asks you to learn a sequence of clicks a think a bit about what analyses you want to run. Is it an alternative equally good than scripting the analyses yourself? No, but it is better than faking proficiency in a language you don't know. It is better than obtaining results you have no way to verify. It is better than providing code you cannot read or explain. We can have a debate of GUI vs code, but if you do not side for code, stick to your guns. Side for the GUI and be a true supporter.
