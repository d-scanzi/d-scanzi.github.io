My Teaching Philosophy

A class is a community where personal experiences, expertise, ideas and personalities meet to create and share knowledge. As an educator, my role is to foster this community and guide the development of understanding. I use the word “guide” with intention. In my view, teaching is not a passive transmission of information from an authority, but a cooperative moment where students contribute actively, contributing to the principle of whanaungatanga.

In class, I strive to establish a shallow yet balanced authority gradient between myself and the students. I achieve this through different strategies, but class and group discussions play a vital role, so I try to use them whenever possible. During class discussions I sit with students, while during small group activities, I spend time with each group individually. Doing so reduces the perceived authority associated with a professor standing in front of the class. This leads to students feeling more comfortable speaking up, sharing their thoughts, asking and answering questions.

A key benefit of establishing a supportive and engaging environment is that students’ confidence in their abilities increases, allowing them to tackle challenging tasks. I have high expectations of all students, and I believe these expectations can be met in the right circumstances. For instance, with a colleague I developed a series of neurophysiological laboratories. We designed the content to be just outside the boundaries of what taught in class. Crucially, we made the laboratories not examinable, which helped students to deepen their understanding without the stress of formal evaluations.

By defining a class as a community, I recognise that each class is unique, with its specific dynamics, requirements, and emerging properties. In class, I actively seek feedback from students, and I modify the details of my methods accordingly to provide adequate care and support, upholding the principle of manākitanga.

During my PhD I have successfully applied my teaching philosophy across different campuses and subjects within and outside the field of psychology. Moreover, I have striven to build communities of students with the goal of increasing the technical and analytical capacity within Te Kura Mātai Hinengaro / School of Psychology. Throughout these experiences, the mix of shallow authority gradient, active learning, and flexibility has been positively welcomed by students, as evidenced in their feedback available here.

My expertise lies at the intersection of psychology, research methodology, and data analysis. As a teacher, I aim to help students to (a) understand how methods guide and limit the interpretation of psychological data, (b) develop the skills needed to transform quantitative and qualitative evidence into shareable knowledge, and (c) provide mutual support. To achieve these goals, I will balance theoretical instruction with in-class, unmarked activities and longer assessed projects. These tasks will give students hands-on experience in deciding the best methods to test a hypothesis, identify ethical limitations, and select appropriate analytical and reporting tools. In the long run, I would like to complement the content by teaching research skills that are often overlooked, like simulating data, writing simple scripts, generating reproducible research, and effective reference management. Whenever possible, I will support the use of freely available open-source tools, to allow students to retain access to their knowledge and skills beyond their university life.

Additionally, I am committed in collaborating across the School to contribute to the development and delivery of teaching material and initiatives. Specifically, I can contribute to the strengthening and innovating of psychological areas such as perception and attention, experimental design, cognitive neuroscience, electrophysiology, neuroanatomy and programming.

Knowing that methods, design and statistics can often feel daunting, I will leverage my experience as a magician and circus performer to deliver engaging classes, find creative ways to teach psychology, and support a high-quality student experience. In doing so, I aim to encourage students’ attendance and participation. As reported by former students, being an energetic and entertaining educator increases their motivation to participate in class.
